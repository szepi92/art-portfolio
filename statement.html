ABOUT

My work was inspired by the strange experience of dreaming in English for the very first time. I consider that night, when the English language entered my unconscious mind, my milestone for becoming bilingual. The principle of linguistic relativity suggests that thought is a consequence of language. If that is true, how do shifts in language immigrants experience as they acquire the common tongue of their new home and gradually lose their native tongue alter the way they think?

My work investigates the fluidity of identity in the context of cultural displacement and changes in language. I am interested in uncovering the old and the new, the struggle to hold onto what was and the thrill of becoming something else, and the awe of balancing two worlds. Through the use of a computer program that measures the reaction time to certain images, I document the duration of thought in different languages. The works are representations of this data, exploring the complexity of the working mind through various processes and diverse mediums. Language becomes portraiture, reflecting upon its unique and vital role in our lives.
